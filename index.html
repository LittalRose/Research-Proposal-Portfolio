Who I Am
A researcher driven by curiosity about people in their natural habitats.
I don't just observe what people click. I watch what they endure, what they abandon, and what they keep trying for anyway. What started as coursework became something more personal: a genuine fascination with the gap between what environments promise and what they actually deliver.
Two environments. Two studies. One thread connecting them: people arriving with genuine intention, and design failing to meet them there.

The short version
Two young women. One app. Real frustration underneath a very specific kind of loyalty. They keep using Posh, not because it works, but because nothing else has everything they need yet. That tension is the research signal.
Research Approach
Secondary research before a single question.
App Store reviews, Trustpilot, Google Play, and social media were analyzed before composing the interview guide. This surfaced four recurring themes: social proof, technical unreliability, transparency gaps, and safety concerns. Each became a thread to pull during the interview.
App Store reviews → FaceTime interview → Design direction
The Interview Setting
6PM. A library study room. She had something to say.
She leaned forward when making a strong argument, raised her eyebrows at suspicious content, and periodically averted her gaze to find the right words for a frustrating memory. At times, the atmosphere seemed sincere, almost confessional. My job was to listen more than talk.
In Their Own Words
"You're going blind. You're unaware about what's going on. You just get your ticket. That's it."
Interview 1, Tool vs. Destination
"I'm not going to some event that I have no idea about. I don't know who's going. It's different for me because I even worry about my safety."
Interview 1, Safety & Trust
"If Posh added that community layer, I'd use it a lot more. Right now I only keep it because it has a wide event catalog."
Interview 2, Unfulfilled Desire
Key Insight
Beneath the frustration was unfulfilled desire, not disengagement.
She didn't want to leave Posh. She wanted a better version of it. That conflict between optimism and disappointment is the exact signal excellent design is meant to respond to.
Three Themes That Surfaced
Theme 01
Transparency
Knowing the price, the crowd, and the venue reality are prerequisites, not luxuries, for this demographic.
High priority
Theme 02
Safety
Both interviewees stitch social context from Instagram, friends, and rival apps because Posh offers none of it.
High priority
Theme 03
Technical Reliability
One ticket failure at the venue door destroys all trust built during the purchase experience.
High priority

The Findings
6 themes emerged from the data.
Every theme is anchored to a direct user quote. Nothing assumed. Everything heard.
High
Tool Not a Destination
Users arrive with a task, complete it, and leave. There's no pull to stay, browse, or return. Posh functions like Google for events, not a community.
High
Social Transparency & Crowd Visibility
Buying a ticket means buying into a crowd, but Posh shows users nothing about who's going. Plots already solves this, and it's taking users.
High
Safety & Trust
For young women, an unknown event is a perceived physical risk, not a social inconvenience. This distinction is critical to understand.
High
Technical Reliability
The app fails at the single most critical moment: the venue door. One crash destroys all trust built during purchase.
Med
UI/UX & App Experience
The interface feels like a database. Users expect TikTok energy. They get a spreadsheet.
Med
Personalization & Social Identity
Users want to be people, not anonymous transactions. A profile transforms the app from a vending machine into a community.

The short version
A park designed for motion, not rest. People arrive wanting to stay, and leave within 12 minutes. The design failure is quiet and invisible until you sit down and watch it happen over and over again.
Driving up, the first thing that hits me is the sound, a natural playlist mixing with city noise. But near the overflowing trash bins, the smell hits immediately. What strikes me most is the contrast between motion and pause. Joggers, cyclists, and dog walkers are in constant flow, while those seeking stillness are just trying to find a quiet spot.

05, Critique, PlanBit Habit App
Case Study Critique
A critical analysis of PlanBit, a habit-tracking app built around a digital garden metaphor. Examined across four dimensions: user research, information architecture, visual design, and evaluation.
Subject
PlanBit Habit App
Framework
Hook Model
Timeline
4-Week Sprint
⬡
The short version
This team saw habit formation as an emotional problem in a market full of apps treating it as a logistical one. The garden metaphor is genuinely beautiful. The gaps are instructive: they reveal the difference between an excellent student effort and a research output of professional caliber.
As you grow, so does the garden. When you slip, it suffers. It greets your return. That is a relationship, and the ultimate goal of user-centered design is to build for that distinction.
From the Case Study Critique
What Works
Multi-method rigor
Hook Model, competitive analysis of 6 apps, 20+ interviews. Most 4-week projects fall far short of this.
Narrative coherence
The Hook Model introduced on page one becomes the reward loop visible on the last screen. Rare and deliberate.
Candid reflection
80% of project time spent on literature research that proved less useful than user interviews. That honesty gives the case study legitimacy.
Gaps Identified
No longitudinal validation
The core hypothesis: does a growing visual garden actually sustain motivation over time? Never tested.
Visual process undocumented
The final identity is unique and deliberate. The journey there is invisible. No mood boards, no rejected directions.
No accessibility consideration
No mention of contrast ratios, touch targets, or screen reader support for a wellness app targeting anxious users.
06, Synthesis & Next Steps
Conclusion, Recommendations, and Where We Go Next
Two environments. Two studies. One thread. What the research revealed, what it demands, and the lightbulb moments that point forward.
The Thesis, Revisited
The problem is never a lack of desire. It is always a failure of the environment to meet it, whether that environment is an app or a park.
Study One, Posh App
She arrives ready to commit. The platform leaves her guessing.
Kate opens Posh with genuine intention. She wants to find something that matches her energy, buy a ticket, and show up with her people. Hidden fees, no social proof, and a crashing app push her back out the door before she gets there.
"I look for events on Posh, and then I get them on Plots."
The clearest signal that intention survived, but the platform didn't.
Recommendations, Posh App
01. Add a "Who's Going" social layer
Attendee thumbnails, age range, and mutual connections visible before purchase. Social confidence replaces blind commitment.
02. Safety Mode with verified organizer badges
Browse crowd data anonymously. Verified badge on every event. Community reviews visible before purchase. Trust built into the design, not bolted on.
03. Offline-first ticket wallet
Auto-cache tickets immediately on purchase. The most stressful moment of the night, the venue door, must become the smoothest. Reliability is a trust mechanism.
04. Transparent pricing from first view
Show total cost on the event card, not just at checkout. Every hidden fee is a trust withdrawal. Stop making users feel deceived right before they commit.
Study Two, Lake Baldwin Park
She arrives ready to stay. The space sends her home in 12 minutes.
The park visitor packs a book, finds the perfect afternoon, and arrives with every intention of remaining. Mosquitoes, overflowing bins, and no shade end her afternoon before it begins. The intention was real. The environment failed it.
It's not enough to count benches or measure distances. You have to see the disappointment on someone's face when they can't find a place to sit.
From the ethnographic field notes, Lake Baldwin Park
Recommendations, Lake Baldwin Park
01. Shade structures & mosquito-repellent landscaping
Solar-powered mosquito control and citronella/lavender plantings near seating areas. Allow the yoga practitioner to finally complete her routine in peace.
02. Expand waste stations from 6 to 12 with smart monitoring
Remove the impossible choice dog owners face between responsibility and convenience. Responsible behavior needs infrastructure that meets it.
03. Outdoor fitness installations in the open field
Validate the creativity of people improvising workouts on benches and tree branches. Give them infrastructure that matches their dedication.
04. Redesign the grassy field as a destination, not a gap
Shade, water features, and flexible seating could transform the emptiest space in the park into its most used. The field doesn't lack visitors. It lacks a reason to stay.
Full Circle
Two methods. Two environments. One insight.
The value of this portfolio is not just the findings. It's in the range of methods used to reach them. Empathy interviews surface what users say and feel. Ethnographic observation captures what they do and endure. Thematic coding traces raw language to design direction. Case study critique identifies what separates a good project from a rigorous one.
Curiosity drove every step. From secondary research before a single interview question, to sitting in a park on a Saturday morning watching a woman check three benches and leave, this work is about showing up, paying attention, and not leaving until you understand why.
