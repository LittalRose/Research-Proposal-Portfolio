Research Context
Listening before asking.
Before composing a single interview question, secondary research was conducted across Trustpilot, Google Play, the Apple App Store, and social media — uncovering recurring themes that shaped the interview guide. This approach ensured questions were specific and grounded, not generic.
"You're going blind. You're unaware about what's going on. You just get your ticket. That's it."
— Interview Participant, Age 20 · Posh App User
The Interview Setting
Context shapes candor.
The interview was conducted at 6PM — when daylight fades and a college campus shifts toward the night. The participant entered a library soundproof room. The researcher was at home, at a desk. Even through a screen, nonverbal cues were present: leaning forward on strong points, raised eyebrows at suspicious content, gaze averted to find words for frustrating memories.
The strategy was to listen more than talk — using transitional phrases like "that actually brings me to my next question" to follow momentum without interrupting flow. A neutral, inquisitive tone was held throughout. No agreement or criticism was expressed.
Key Quotes
In their own words.
"I'm not going to some event that I have no idea about. I don't know who's going. It's different for me because I even worry about my safety."
— Interview 1 · Safety & Trust
"I look for events on Posh, and then I get them on Plots."
— Interview 1 · Platform Switching
"There's no way to tell if an event is actually good just from looking at it — no ratings, no comments, nothing."
— Interview 2 · Transparency Gap
"If Posh added that community layer, I'd use it a lot more. Right now I only keep it because it has a wide event catalog."
— Interview 2 · Unfulfilled Desire
Analysis
Three themes surfaced.
Theme 01
Transparency
Users need to feel informed before making a commitment. Knowing the price, the crowd, and the venue reality are not luxuries — they are prerequisites for this demographic.
High Priority
Theme 02
Safety
Both interviewees are young women navigating unfamiliar venues at night. Posh offers no community layer, no attendee visibility. Users stitch context from Instagram and friends.
High Priority
Theme 03
Technical Reliability
Tickets fail at venue doors. Profiles refuse to upload. The app feels unfinished relative to competitors — and one crash destroys all trust built during purchase.
High Priority
The most unexpected revelation was emotional rather than factual: beneath the frustration was unfulfilled desire, not disengagement. She wanted a better app. That conflict between optimism and disappointment is the exact signal excellent design is meant to respond to.

The Persona
Kate Bon — she never buys alone.
Kate is a 20-year-old student and barista who browses Posh every evening but almost always discovers events via Instagram or friends first. She drops event links into group chats, waits for reactions, checks who else is going — and only then considers buying. Hidden fees at checkout and app crashes destroy her trust immediately.

Empathy Map
What she sees, hears, feels.
What She Sees
Event-packed social feeds · Group chats buzzing with links · Fees that only appear at the very last checkout step · "Is this worth it? Will my friends actually come?"
What She Hears
"Is everyone going?" — constantly from her circle · Friends venting about glitchy apps · "Did you buy tickets yet?" — FOMO pressure · "I want something real."

The persona was built from two themes that emerged across both interviews: the desire for social certainty before commitment, and the fear that the environment — digital or physical — will let her down.

The Findings
6 themes emerged from the data.
Every theme is anchored to a direct user quote. Nothing assumed. Everything heard.
High
Tool Not a Destination
Users arrive with a task, complete it, and leave. There's no pull to stay, browse, or return. Posh functions like Google for events — not a community.
High
Social Transparency & Crowd Visibility
Buying a ticket means buying into a crowd — but Posh shows users nothing about who's going. Plots already solves this, and it's taking users.
High
Safety & Trust
For young women, an unknown event is a perceived physical risk — not a social inconvenience. This distinction is critical for this demographic.
High
Technical Reliability
The app fails at the single most critical moment: the venue door. One crash destroys all trust built during the purchase experience.
Med
UI/UX & App Experience
The interface feels like a database. Users expect the energy of TikTok — they get a spreadsheet. No visual pull to stay.
Med
Personalization & Social Identity
Users want to be people, not anonymous transactions. A profile transforms the app from a vending machine into a community.
3 Non-Negotiables
What the data demands.
Safety Is a Feature, Not a Footnote
Young women will not use a platform they cannot trust. Crowd transparency, verified organizers, and privacy controls are not optional — they are table stakes for this demographic. Design for safety first. Everything else follows.
The Social Layer Is the Product
Posh is losing users to Plots for exactly one reason: social visibility. Until users can see who is going before they buy, the app will always be a stepping stone rather than a destination. The social layer is not a feature — it is the entire product.
Reliability at the Door Is Non-Negotiable
One app failure at the venue entrance destroys every ounce of trust built during the purchase experience. Offline ticket caching isn't a nice-to-have — it's a trust mechanism. The most stressful moment of the night must become the smoothest.

The Research Question
How do visitors actually use this space?
How do visitors in Lake Baldwin Park utilize the outdoor recreational space to meet their physical, social, and wellness needs — and what design changes could better encourage ongoing, stationary activities? The focus was specifically on those who arrive intending to stay: the reader, the yogi, the meditator. The ones who are not joggers passing through, but people trying to be still in a space not designed for stillness.
Driving up to the park, the first thing that hits is the sound — like a natural playlist mixing with city noise. But near the overflowing trash bins, the smell hits immediately. What strikes me most is the contrast between motion and pause.
— Field Notes, Lake Baldwin Park
Observations & Findings
Three moments of failure.
01
12 min
Average time before abandonment · Environmental discomfort
Time and again, people settled in with books or yoga mats — genuinely wanting to spend quality time in the park — only to abandon their spots within 10–15 minutes due to relentless mosquitoes. One woman's yoga practice lasted just twelve minutes before the insects won. The spacious grassy field, which could accommodate so many activities, remained almost completely empty.

The Subject
PlanBit — habit as emotion, not logistics.
PlanBit is a habit-tracking app built around a digital garden metaphor: when you maintain a habit, your garden flourishes; neglect causes it to wither. This team saw habit formation as an emotional problem in a market full of apps treating it as a logistical one. The critique examines how well the case study documents and supports that insight.
As you grow, so does the garden. When you slip, it suffers. It greets your return. It's not a set of features. That is a relationship — and the ultimate goal of user-centered design is to build for that distinction.
— Case Study Critique
What Works
Strengths worth recognizing.
Research
Multi-Method Rigor
Hook Model foundation, competitive analysis of 6 apps, 20+ interviews with graduates and early professionals. The research phase outperforms most 4-week projects.
Strong
Narrative
Intellectual Coherence
The Hook Model introduced on page one becomes the reward loop visible on the last screen. Persona pain points recur in every major design decision. Rare coherence.
Strong
Iteration
Candid Reflection
The team acknowledged spending 80% of time on literature research that turned out less relevant than user interviews. That kind of honesty gives the case study legitimacy.
Authentic
Gaps Identified
What the critique surfaced.
No longitudinal validation
The core hypothesis — that a growing visual garden sustains motivation over time — is never tested. The most important question the study raised remains unanswered.
Visual exploration underdocumented
The final visual identity is unique and deliberate, but the process that led to it is invisible. No mood boards, no rejected directions, no explanation of why the garden over other metaphors.
Usability testing lacks demographic context
Five rounds of testing are described, but participant demographics, recruitment methods, and whether participants were consistent across rounds are never disclosed.
No accessibility consideration
No mention of contrast ratios, touch target sizing, screen reader support, or reduced-motion settings — significant oversight for a wellness app targeting users who may include people with ADHD, anxiety, or visual processing differences.

Finding
Intention vs. Environment
The Research Thesis
The problem is never a lack of desire. It is always a failure of the environment to meet it — whether that environment is an app, a park, or a habit system.
Study One
Posh — she arrives ready to commit.
Kate opens the app with genuine intention to find something that matches her energy, buy a ticket, and show up with her people. Hidden fees, no social proof, a crashing app, and no community layer push her back out the door before she ever gets there. The intention was real. The environment failed it.
"I look for events on Posh, and then I get them on Plots."
— The clearest signal that intention survived, but the platform didn't.
Study Two
Lake Baldwin — she arrives ready to stay.
The park visitor packs a book, finds the perfect afternoon, and arrives with every intention of staying. Mosquitoes end her visit in 12 minutes. A full grassy field — with not a single shade structure — remains empty. One woman checks three benches before leaving entirely. The intention was real. The environment failed it.
It's not enough to count benches or measure distances — you have to see the disappointment on someone's face when they can't find a place to sit.
— Ethnographic Field Notes, Lake Baldwin Park
What This Demonstrates
Two methods. One researcher.
The value of this portfolio is not just in the findings themselves — it's in the range of methods used to reach them. Empathy interviews surface what users say and feel. Ethnographic observation captures what they do and endure. Thematic coding traces the pattern from raw language to design direction. Case study critique identifies the gap between a good project and a rigorous one.
